Creator(s)
Richard Stallman is the creator of the Free Software movement. Since he also is the main author of several "copyleft" licenses (including the GNU/GPL), he is also a lead creator and contributor to the Open Source movement.

Another important contributor to the Open Source movement, Eric Raymond is credited with writing one of the first academic articles concerning the hacker culture and its process of 'distributed peer review' - a fancy way of saying "Hackers use open source".

Creation of the open source movement
The free software movement started in the early 1983 as a result of Richard Stallman's founding of the GNU Operating System.

Later, in 1985, the Free Software Foundation was founded to support the movement.

In early 1990, the GNU Operating System was 99% completed, with all but the kernel having been completed.

In 1992, Linus Torvald's Linux kernel attracted the attention of the GNU Project. A few months later, Torvald relicensed the kernel under the GNU/GPL, officially 'completing' the GNU Operating System and creating the first open source operating system.

In 1997, Eric Raymond published The Cathedral and the Bazaar, an analysis of the hacker community and mindset and the principles of free software. Netscape Communcations took significant interest in the paper.

In 1998, Netscape Communcations - the corporation behind the Netscape browser - announced that they would be releasing the source code of their browser as free software. Being the first widely publicized release of free software, the free software movement met to decide on how to further spread their message while 'piggybacking' on Netscape's big press release.

At the meeting, the term 'free software' was deemed too confusing for the general public, and because of this, the movement decided that they needed a new name.
That name was 'Open Source'.

After the meeting, Richard Stallman decided against adopting the term, and later distanced himself from the open source movement on grounds that the movement had strayed too far from the initial ideas behind free information.

Richard Stallman still supports the open source movement, however, citing the reasoning that both movements are against proprietary software as his main reason for accepting the open source movement.
