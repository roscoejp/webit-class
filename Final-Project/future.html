Future of the open source movement
The future of the open source movement is, as it always has been, controversial.

There are, among other things, open source evangelists that predict the entire world will be using open source applications in the coming decades.

There are even more people who believe - or hope - that open source will never have the momentum to overtake proprietary manufacturers.

And then you have everyone else. You see, everyone figures that open source will be useful, as it is now, in web-based applications. Things like servers and databases and the like.

Everyone else also figures that some people will just never bother switching to open source software. Some people are just use to using Windows and Mac.

Everyone else also knows that, even though the open source movement will keep on growing, it will never be able to completely rid the world of proprietary software.

Everyone else has been learning and studying and watching open source, sometimes knowingly, and sometimes unknowingly. So everyone has a pretty good idea of whether or not they like the idea of OSS.

And everyone else has noticed that there are more and more open source software vendors moving mainstream. Bug Labs in New York and Google are a few of the bigger organisations that have started providing open source materials. So it makes sense that, if they can survive the next few years, why shouldn't other companies be able to weather the burden of surviving not on profit, but on praise (and a few donations).

Hopefully you'll agree, and join everyone else in knowing that open source will keep on growing. And no matter what anyone desires or does, will be around for a very long time.
